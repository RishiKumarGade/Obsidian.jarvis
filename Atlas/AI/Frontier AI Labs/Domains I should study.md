
This is exactly how I'd structure it if I were designing a **10-year Frontier AI Engineer curriculum**. The goal is **not** to learn everything in computer science or physics. It's to master the domains that compound into becoming an exceptional AI engineer and researcher.

I've separated them into **Core**, **Supporting**, and **Specialization** domains. The order is roughly the dependency graph, not necessarily the order you'll study them.

---

# Tier 1 — Core Foundations (Must Master)

These are non-negotiable. Every one of your target roles depends on them.

---

## 1. Mathematics

**Purpose:** The language of AI.

**Summary**  
The mathematical foundation behind machine learning, optimization, probability, and modern deep learning. You'll develop intuition for why algorithms work instead of treating them as black boxes.

**Major Areas**

	- Linear Algebra
	    
	- Calculus
	    
	- Probability
	    
	- Statistics
	    
	- Optimization
	    
	- Numerical Methods
	    
	- Information Theory
	    
	- Discrete Mathematics
	    

---

## 2. Computer Science

**Purpose:** Learn how computers actually solve problems.

**Summary**  
Covers algorithms, complexity, memory, computation, and programming fundamentals. This teaches you to reason about efficiency and correctness.

**Major Areas**

- Data Structures
    
- Algorithms
    
- Complexity Theory
    
- Automata & Computation
    
- Programming Paradigms
    
- Software Engineering
    
- Design Patterns
    

---

## 3. Systems Programming

**Purpose:** Understand what happens below Python.

**Summary**  
Modern AI runs on low-level software. This domain explains memory, processes, operating systems, and high-performance programming.

**Major Areas**

- C/C++
    
- Memory Management
    
- Operating Systems
    
- Linux
    
- Multithreading
    
- Concurrency
    
- Networking
    
- IPC
    
- Debugging
    
- Performance Profiling
    

---

## 4. Artificial Intelligence

**Purpose:** Understand AI beyond deep learning.

**Summary**  
The broader field of intelligent systems, including search, planning, reasoning, knowledge representation, and decision making.

**Major Areas**

- Classical AI
    
- Search Algorithms
    
- Knowledge Representation
    
- Planning
    
- Constraint Solving
    
- Expert Systems
    
- Agents
    

---

## 5. Machine Learning

**Purpose:** Learn how machines learn from data.

**Summary**  
The foundations of supervised, unsupervised, and reinforcement learning, model evaluation, feature engineering, and generalization.

**Major Areas**

- Regression
    
- Classification
    
- Clustering
    
- Trees
    
- Ensembles
    
- SVMs
    
- Reinforcement Learning
    
- Model Evaluation
    

---

## 6. Deep Learning

**Purpose:** Learn neural networks from first principles.

**Summary**  
The mathematics, architectures, optimization, and training of deep neural networks.

**Major Areas**

- Neural Networks
    
- CNNs
    
- RNNs
    
- LSTMs
    
- Attention
    
- Transformers
    
- Diffusion Models
    
- Graph Neural Networks
    

---

## 7. Foundation Models & Generative AI

**Purpose:** Build and improve modern AI models.

**Summary**  
Everything related to LLMs, multimodal models, training pipelines, post-training, and deployment.

**Major Areas**

- LLMs
    
- Tokenization
    
- Pretraining
    
- Fine-tuning
    
- RLHF
    
- DPO
    
- RAG

- Long Context
    
- Model Evaluation

**Major Areas**

- Distributed Algorithms
    
- Consensus
    
- CAP Theorem
    
- RPC
    
- Message Queues
    
- Distributed Training
    
- Fault Tolerance

- CUDA

- Profiling
    
- Compiler Optimizations

# Tier 4 — Supporting Sciences

These are not mandatory for every AI engineer, but they provide intuition and open doors to advanced research.

---

## 16. Physics

**Purpose:** Develop mathematical intuition and analytical thinking.

**Summary**  
Physics strengthens reasoning about dynamic systems, optimization, probability, and modeling. It is especially valuable if you're interested in robotics, simulation, or scientific AI.

**Major Areas**

- Classical Mechanics
    
- Electricity & Magnetism
    
- Waves
    
- Optics
    
- Thermodynamics
    
- Quantum Mechanics (introductory)
    
- Statistical Mechanics (later, if interested)
    

---

## 17. Electronics & Computer Hardware

**Purpose:** Understand the hardware executing AI workloads.

**Summary**  
Learn how processors, memory, digital logic, and hardware accelerators function, providing context for GPU programming and system optimization.

**Major Areas**

- Digital Electronics
    
- Computer Organization
    
- Logic Design
    
- Processor Architecture
    
- Memory Systems
    
- FPGA Basics (optional)
    

---

## 18. Cognitive Science & Neuroscience

**Purpose:** Understand natural intelligence.

**Summary**  
Study how humans perceive, learn, remember, and reason. This can inspire AI architectures and evaluation methods.

**Major Areas**

- Learning
    
- Memory
    
- Attention
    
- Perception
    
- Decision Making
    
- Language Processing
    

---

## One refinement I'd make

I would add one more domain that ties everything together:

### 26. **Engineering Projects & Open Source**

This isn't a subject—it's where you _prove_ your skills.

Throughout your journey, you'll:

- Reproduce influential papers.
    
- Build training and inference systems.
    
- Contribute to projects like PyTorch, vLLM, or Hugging Face.
    
- Publish your own research and tooling.
    
- Develop a portfolio that demonstrates mastery.
    

That portfolio becomes the evidence that matters to frontier AI companies, often more than the name of your university.
